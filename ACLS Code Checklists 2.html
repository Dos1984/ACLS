ECG Rhythm Reference
Not sure what you're looking at? Compare the monitor against these example strips. Tap a category to jump to it.
Cardiac Arrest \u2014 Shockable vs. Non-Shockable
Ventricular Fibrillation \u2014 SHOCKABLE
Chaotic waveform of totally irregular timing and amplitude; no identifiable P, QRS, or T. Defibrillate immediately \u2014 never try to synchronize a shock to this rhythm.
Pulseless V-Tach \u2014 SHOCKABLE
Regular, wide, monomorphic complexes at a very fast rate, giving a classic smooth "sine-wave" look. If there is no pulse, treat exactly like VF.
Asystole \u2014 NOT shockable
Flat or near-flat line. Before calling it: confirm in a second lead and check that the leads are attached and the gain isn't turned down (rule out fine VF).
Organized Rhythm (context: PEA) \u2014 NOT shockable
Any organized-looking rhythm \u2014 even one this normal \u2014 with no palpable pulse is PEA. The diagnosis is made at the bedside, not on the monitor.
Fast Rhythms \u2014 Identifying Atrial Fibrillation
Normal Sinus Rhythm (for comparison)
Perfectly regular; one identical P wave before every QRS; constant PR interval. This is the baseline to compare against.
Atrial Fibrillation
Irregularly irregular R-R intervals; no discrete P waves \u2014 just a wavy, fibrillatory baseline; QRS is usually narrow.
SVT (for contrast)
Regular, narrow, fast rhythm with P waves typically hidden in the preceding T wave. Unlike AFib, the R-R spacing is perfectly regular.
Slow Rhythms \u2014 Sinus Bradycardia & AV Blocks
Sinus Bradycardia
Normal P-QRS-T sequence and a normal, constant PR interval \u2014 just a slow regular rate. Every beat looks like a textbook complex.
1st-Degree AV Block
Every P wave conducts to a QRS, but the PR interval is fixed and prolonged (>0.20 sec). Rhythm otherwise stays regular.
2nd-Degree, Mobitz I (Wenckebach)
The PR interval lengthens with each beat until a P wave is dropped entirely \u2014 look for "grouped beating" with R waves creeping closer together before the pause.
2nd-Degree, Mobitz II
PR interval stays constant on every conducted beat, then a P wave is suddenly not followed by a QRS \u2014 no warning, no progressive lengthening. Higher risk of progressing to complete block.
3rd-Degree (Complete) AV Block
P waves march out at their own steady rate; QRS complexes march out at their own separate, slower steady rate. Neither relates to the other (AV dissociation) \u2014 the QRS is often wide, reflecting a ventricular escape rhythm.
Reference only \u2014 always correlate with the patient's pulse, blood pressure, and clinical status.
